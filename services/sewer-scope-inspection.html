Common Issues Found During Sewer Scopes


  • Root Intrusion
  • Pipe "Bellies" or Sagging
  • Cracked or Collapsed Lines
    •

Identifying these underground issues early can save thousands of dollars in excavation and landscape restoration costs.

Root Intrusion

Tree roots are naturally drawn to the moisture in sewer lines. Even small cracks can allow roots to enter, where they grow rapidly, causing major blockages and eventually destroying the pipe.


Low Spots or "Bellies"

A "belly" occurs when a section of the pipe sags due to soil erosion or improper installation. This causes water and waste to pool, leading to frequent backups and debris buildup.


Separated or Cracked Joints

Over time, shifting soil can cause pipe joints to pull apart or crack. This allows sewage to leak into the surrounding soil and creates a catch-point for toilet paper and solids.

Frequently Asked Questions About Sewer Scopes

Should I get a sewer scope on a new construction home?

Yes. Construction debris, such as rocks or wood, can accidentally fall into lines during builds, and heavy machinery can crush pipes before the home is even finished.


Is a sewer scope part of a standard home inspection?

No. Most home inspections only check if drains are currently flowing. A scope is an add-on service that looks at the actual condition of the buried pipes.


What happens if you find a problem?

We provide a video link and a written report. You can use this documentation to get repair estimates from plumbers or negotiate repairs with a home seller.
